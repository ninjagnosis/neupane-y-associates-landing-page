## When VAT Becomes Your Responsibility

VAT (Value-Added Tax) is Nepal's primary goods-and-services tax. Below a certain threshold, you're exempt. Above it, you're required to register, collect VAT from customers, and remit it to the IRD. Get this wrong and penalties pile up fast.

### Registration Threshold

If your annual revenue exceeds **NRs 5 Million**, VAT registration is mandatory.

Below that threshold, registration is *optional* — but sometimes strategic. Why register early? Mostly because you can recover VAT on your purchases immediately, which improves cash flow if you're buying equipment or raw materials.

### What Happens When You Register

Once registered:
- You collect 13% VAT from customers (standard rate; some goods are exempt or reduced-rate)
- You file a monthly VAT return and remit net VAT to the IRD by the 15th of the next month
- You maintain detailed records of all purchases and sales
- Your financial statements now reflect a new liability: *VAT Payable*

### Common Registration Mistakes

**Registering late**: You're legally liable for VAT from the day you cross the threshold, not from the day you register. Backdated liability can be substantial.

**Not tracking invoicing**: Every invoice must show your VAT registration number and the VAT charged. Missing invoices = missing records.

**Forgetting exemptions**: Exports, inter-company supplies, and certain financial services don't attract VAT. Charging VAT on these means overpayment.

### Monthly Compliance

1. **Reconcile sales**: Match invoiced sales to ledger totals.
2. **Calculate VAT**: 13% of taxable sales (less exempt sales).
3. **Identify input VAT**: VAT you paid on purchases that can be credited.
4. **File return**: Submit VAT Form 15 by the 15th.
5. **Pay net VAT**: Remit via bank to IRD's VAT account.

Missing a deadline? Expect a 10% penalty (minimum NRs 500) plus interest.

### Input VAT: The Game-Changer

If you paid VAT on purchases (from suppliers, import duties, services), you can *recover* that VAT.  
For example:
- You import goods worth NRs 100,000 → You pay NRs 13,000 VAT
- You sell those goods for NRs 130,000 → You collect NRs 16,900 VAT
- You owe the IRD: 16,900 – 13,000 = NRs 3,900

**But**: You need proper invoices documenting every input. Without an invoice from your supplier, that VAT cannot be recovered.

### Import VAT

Goods imported into Nepal attract VAT at customs. You pay VAT on import, plus customs duties. Document import declarations (BOE — Bill of Entry) carefully — these are your proof of input VAT.

### Common Compliance Failures

- **Failing to register when threshold is crossed**: Arrears + penalties catch up.
- **Filing VAT late**: Automatic penalty every month you're late.
- **Mixing VAT categories**: Some goods are 0%, some 13%. Misclassifying inflates your VAT liability.
- **No audit trail**: Invoices can't be found or matched. IRD disallows the input VAT claim.

### VAT Return Reconciliation

Every VAT return should reconcile to:
- Your general ledger (total sales, total purchases)
- Your bank deposits (cash inflows)
- Your accounts payable aging (money owed to suppliers)

If these don't tie, find the gap *before* filing.

### Deadlines You Can't Miss

- **15th of each month**: Remit prior-month VAT
- **30th of each month**: File prior-month return
- **Amends**: Can be filed within the same fiscal year if errors are discovered

### Next Steps

If you're approaching the NRs 5M threshold or recently crossed it, now is the time to:
1. Engage an accountant familiar with VAT compliance
2. Implement invoice tracking (invoicing software makes this automatic)
3. Organize existing records so input VAT recovery isn't left on the table
